Key Findings

The Headline: Leadership Training Boosts Effectiveness
Students who received approximately 60 minutes of emotional intelligence training and facilitated festival activities showed a significant gain in Effectiveness (+3.48, p = .046, d = 0.29). The control group (first-year attendees) showed essentially no change (−0.03). This suggests that active engagement in emotion-focused facilitation meaningfully improves perceived effectiveness—a critical competency for emerging leaders and educators.
The Protective Pattern: Buffering Normal Decline
Control students typically declined across most measures (a common pattern in semester retesting, especially from fall to late fall). The experimental group—despite facing the same test-retest challenges—was largely buffered from this decline. They improved on Recognize Patterns (+1.93), Consequential Thinking (+2.23), and Know Yourself (+1.31), while control students stayed flat or declined on these same competencies. This protection effect suggests the EQ intervention built resilience and practical emotional skills.
The Consistency Advantage: All Life Outcomes Favor Treatment
Across all four life outcome measures (Effectiveness, Relationship, Quality of Life, Wellbeing) and Total EQ, the difference-in-differences estimates were positive (ranging from DiD = +1.21 to +3.51), even when confidence intervals overlapped zero. This consistent directionality—despite modest sample size (n = 49 exp, n = 92 ctrl)—points to a genuine, multidimensional benefit of EQ training and active leadership involvement.
Statistical Power and the Sample Size Caveat
With 49 matched experimental pairs, we achieve adequate power for the primary effect (Effectiveness) but not for most secondary comparisons. Most individual competencies do not reach traditional p < .05 significance thresholds. However, the consistency of positive effects across nearly all 16 measures, combined with strong qualitative evidence, suggests the intervention is real—not merely statistical noise. Replication with larger samples would strengthen these claims.
Qualitative Evidence Complements Quantitative Findings
Student reflections emphasize increased confidence, adaptability, leadership skills, and sense of professional identity. Several students noted the experience reduced nervousness and improved their ability to handle emotions "on the fly"—exactly the kind of agility and presence that Emotional Intelligence theory predicts. The fact that second-grade participants internalized the "kindness is cool" message illustrates the ripple effect of emotionally intelligent facilitation.
Implications for Teacher Education and Leadership Development
The POP-UP Festival model demonstrates a scalable, experiential approach to building emotional intelligence and leadership identity in college students. By embedding EQ training and real-world facilitation into a community service event, UD created a high-impact learning moment that moved students from theory to practice. The protective effects on emotional resilience suggest this model could be extended beyond single events into sustained leadership programs.
Methodology: Pre/post pairs matched by email. Statistical tests: paired t-test for within-group change; independent samples t-test for between-group difference-in-differences. Effect sizes: Cohen’s d. Experimental group received ~60 min EQ training and led POP-UP Festival activities; control group attended as participants. Assessment window: Pre-test Aug–Oct 2025, Post-test Oct–Nov 2025. Confidence intervals calculated as DiD ± 1.96 × SE (where SE derived from pooled within-group variance).
